## ğŸ“Š Dimensions Analyzed

### 1. **E-Commerce and Product Suitability**

* Over 85% of Amazon items weigh less than 5 lbs, making them ideal for drones.
* Popular categories for drone delivery include books, OTC medicine, hygiene products, phone accessories, and pet supplies.
* The massive surge in e-commerce, especially post-pandemic, makes last-mile innovations necessary.

### 2. **Drone Capabilities and Cost Savings**

* Prime Air drones can deliver within a 15-mile range and carry packages under 5 lbs.
* Cost reductions of 22â€“27% are possible by replacing van-based last-mile delivery.
* Drones significantly reduce carbon emissions (up to 94% per delivery compared to trucks).
* FAA approvals (e.g., BVLOS) and patented safety innovations pave the way for mass adoption.

### 3. **Demographics and Geography of Massachusetts**

* The population is nearly 7 million, with internet penetration at 93%.
* Boston and Cambridge have high Prime membership rates (~72%) and urban density.
* Western Massachusetts includes rural areas where drones can fill gaps in logistics.
* Cities analyzed: Boston, Cambridge, Worcester, Springfield, Lowell, Pittsfield.

### 4. **Location Selection and Rollout Plan**

A three-phase implementation is recommended:

#### **Phase 1: Springfield**

* Launch in a low-density rural area to test performance and gather safety data.
* Focus on system setup, battery logistics, and airspace navigation.

#### **Phase 2: Worcester**

* Test hybrid models involving drone-to-locker and drone-to-van systems.
* Acts as a bridge between rural and urban environments.

#### **Phase 3: Cambridge**

* Introduce autonomous drone delivery at scale in partnership with FAA and MIT.
* Tap into smart city infrastructure for real-time routing and predictive logistics.

Each site was evaluated based on population, delivery density, flight range, and infrastructure readiness.
